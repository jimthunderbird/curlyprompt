Quantum computing is the use of superposed and entangled quantum states in computers. A quantum computer can be viewed as sampling from quantum systems that evolve in ways operating on an enormous number of possibilities simultaneously, though they remain subject to strict computational constraints. Unlike classical computers which operate according to deterministic rules, quantum computers exploit principles like quantum superposition (where qubits can exist in a linear combination of states) and quantum entanglement.

The basic unit of information in quantum computing is the qubit (quantum bit), which differs from a classical bit because it can exist in multiple states simultaneously through superposition. When measured, a qubit's state collapses to one of its possible values based on probability determined by the Born rule. Quantum algorithms involve creating procedures that use wave interference effects to amplify the probability of desired measurement results.

Quantum computers are theoretically capable of performing some calculations exponentially faster than classical computers, particularly for problems like integer factorization and simulating quantum systems. However, current implementations are largely experimental and only suitable for specialized tasks. They require extremely low temperatures and sophisticated isolation from environmental interference to prevent quantum decoherence, which introduces noise into calculations.

The field emerged from the convergence of quantum mechanics and computer science, with early developments including the quantum Turing machine, quantum algorithms like Shor's algorithm for factoring, and Grover's algorithm for search problems. While quantum computers can theoretically solve any problem that classical computers can, they are suspected to solve certain problems more efficiently, particularly those involving quantum systems or optimization tasks.

Current quantum computers are not yet practical for real-world applications, with most existing systems having limited qubit counts and high error rates, though researchers continue working toward scalable, fault-tolerant quantum computers.
